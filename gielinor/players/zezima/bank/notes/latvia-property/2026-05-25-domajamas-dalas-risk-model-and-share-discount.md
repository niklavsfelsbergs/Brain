# Domājamās daļas — the risk model, the share discount, and the added-floor trap

**Claim.** Extends the S056 notes ([[domajamas-dalas-vs-dzivokla-ipasums]],
[[riga-apartment-buy-evaluation]]) with what the Ģertrūdes 113 deep-dive (S066) pinned down.

**The risk model — categorical vs degree.** Most risks of owning an ideal share are matters of
*degree* and you'd rarely feel them day-to-day: worse liquidity, a permanent discount, coordination
friction, a possible stranger-co-owner via a neighbour's debt (harmless if the use-arrangement is
registered). Only **one** is a difference *in kind*: **forced division (CL 1074)** — any co-owner
can sue to dissolve the co-ownership, and worst case the whole building is auctioned and you're
bought out at ~70–75% of market. You lose *the home*, not the money. With a *dzīvokļa īpašums* this
can never happen; with a share the law always technically permits it. Low probability in a large
*functioning* building (no one with 1/80 gains from it; courts prefer buy-outs; 2022 5-yr bar) — but
it never fully goes away short of conversion, which a big building won't achieve.

**"People buy these and are fine" is real but conditional.** The fine cases are the *checked* ones:
registered *lietošanas kārtība*, a functioning/managed building, financing that exists. The burned
ones hit a financing wall, a deadlocked co-ownership, or needed liquidity. So the due-diligence
gates aren't lawyer-fluff — they're the exact filter between fine and burned. A blanket "avoid
domājamās daļas" is calibrated to the median *unchecked* flat; a checked functioning one usually
sits in the fine bucket.

**The share discount — magnitude finally pinned.** ~a third off clean title (range ~25–45%;
deeper for messy multi-apartment shares, shallower for clean *pusmāja*). Hard comp: **Bruņinieku 28**,
a renovated 1910 Centrs flat explicitly registered as a share, asking €1,860/m² ≈ 38% below the
~€2,900–3,100/m² renovated-pre-war-Centrs benchmark. **This replaces the unreliable ≈90% anecdote.**
Key framing: the discount **is the risk price, not a gift** — buying at the going share price means
you're *not* over-compensated for the risk, and your upside is **capped** vs titled stock (which
appreciates to full market). It can wash if you bought low and hold; it bites if you overpay or need
a fast sale. Pool is still **predominantly cash** — no evidence bank/Altum lending on multi-apartment
shares widened in 2025–26.

**The added-floor trap (new due-diligence item).** If a building had floor area added (a *virsstāva
/ jumta izbūve*), per VZD that **recalculates every co-owner's domājamās daļas**, and per **CL 1068**
the addition required **unanimous co-owner written consent**. Verify: consent obtained, build permit
valid + commissioned, and **shares recalculated and re-registered** in the Zemesgrāmata. A sloppy
addition is a latent defect on what you're buying. (Double-edged: that an addition happened at all is
evidence the co-ownership *can* act collectively.)

**Financing — distrust the seller's agent here.** The selling agent will say "mortgage shouldn't be
a problem." The market reality: Tier-1 banks resist bare multi-apartment shares; Altum's share
financing is *pusmāja*-shaped (2–3 co-owners), not an 80-unit building. Get a **signed bank offer
for the specific share** before emotional commitment — a lender's real yes is also the one validation
that doesn't come from someone selling you the flat.
