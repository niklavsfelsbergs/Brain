## The one-sentence reframe

You would not be buying *an apartment*. You'd be buying an **abstract fraction of a
whole building** (Civillikums 1067/1072) plus a **side-agreement** about which rooms are
yours to use. Everything below flows from that gap.

## Verdict

Latvian professionals lean **"don't — unless"**. A named zvērināts advokāts (Egīls
Radziņš) advises against buying a *domājamā daļa* in a multi-apartment building outright;
the state legal portal says don't buy if the documentation isn't sorted. So the default
posture is caution. Whether *this* apartment escapes the blunt "no" hinges on a small set
of checkable facts — above all **one**.

## The hinge: is there a *lietošanas kārtība* registered in the Zemesgrāmata?

All four researchers converged here independently:
- **Occupancy security (P1):** your right to live in *that specific flat* binds future
  buyers/co-owners **only if the use-arrangement is registered** in the Land Register.
  Unregistered, it's a contract among today's co-owners — a new co-owner who buys another
  share isn't bound (medium-confidence inference, but every source pushes registration).
- **Financing (P2):** banks treat a registered use-arrangement as a de-risker; it's part
  of what makes a share financeable at all.
- **Resale (P3):** its absence is a named resale-depressing factor.
- **Due diligence (P4):** it's the #1 pre-purchase check across every source.

**If it's not registered, that is the first thing to fix or walk from.**

## The second fork: *pusmāja* vs. carved-up multi-apartment building

The research draws a sharp line between two cases that look identical in a listing:
- **Good-ish case — a half-house (*pusmāja*) or few co-owners.** Cleanly divisible,
  financeable (esp. via Altum, which *explicitly* supports buying a *domājamā daļa* of a
  half-house), convertible later with a small number of people to agree.
- **"Time bomb" case — an apartment that is really an undivided share of a
  multi-apartment building.** Banks resist; conversion needs everyone; this is the case
  practitioners call *"bumba ar laika degli."*

Which one this is changes the answer materially. **Need to know: how many co-owners /
apartments, and is the building split into proper units or not.**

## Risks that remain even with everything registered

1. **Anyone can force division at any time (CL 1074).** A co-owner can sue to dissolve the
   co-ownership; if no agreement, the court can order the **whole building auctioned at
   ~70–75% of market value** and you're bought out — you lose the home, get cash. A 2022
   reform adds a 5-year bar with *broad, discretionary exceptions*; after 5 years the right
   is open-ended. This exposure is structural and never fully goes away short of conversion.
2. **A co-owner's creditors can auction *their* share — and pre-emption does NOT apply at a
   forced auction.** A stranger (investor, debt-buyer) can become your co-owner with no say
   from you.
3. **Joint decisions need consent.** Renovations, renting out, major repairs — one
   uncooperative co-owner can block them, no fast remedy short of court.
4. **Costs.** Post-SKC-108/2025 (Dec 2025 Senāts): *usage* utilities fall on whoever lives
   there; management/reserve-fund + property tax stay split by share regardless of use.
   A non-paying co-owner's debt can still practically land on the property.

## Financing reality (P2)

- Legally you *can* mortgage your own share without the others' consent (CL 1072); on
  default only your share is auctioned.
- But **Tier-1 banks generally won't write a normal mortgage against a bare ideal share** —
  no bank publishes a hard rule; this is practitioner-consensus inference. The realistic
  path is an **Altum-guaranteed loan** through a partner bank, which fits best for a clean
  *pusmāja* **with a registered use-arrangement**.
- Fallback: non-bank secured lenders (~80% LTV, ~€250k cap) at worse terms.
- LTV/rate premium specific to ideal shares: unquantified — **call a bank/broker desk.**

## Resale & the escape hatch (P3)

- Shares sell **below** equivalent registered apartments — direction unanimous, magnitude
  poorly sourced (don't trust the ≈90% anecdote; realistic read is "meaningful, tens-of-%,"
  driven by the mortgage problem shrinking the buyer pool to cash buyers).
- Exit friction: 2-month first-refusal for co-owners + a **1-year redemption clawback**
  after sale to an outsider — keeps title "unclean" for a year, deters buyers.
- **The cure: convert to *dzīvokļa īpašums*.** VZD division process. VZD fees are cheap
  (~€40 opinion + ~€30–45/apartment); the **cadastral survey** (if not already done) is the
  variable, possibly dominant cost. **Requires unanimous consent of ALL co-owners** — one
  holdout halts it (cooperative owners become "hostages"). If blocked, the only route is a
  court action for forced dissolution (slow, adversarial). Conversion is what turns a
  cash-only discounted share back into a normal liquid apartment.

## Decision frame

**Lean WALK if:** it's an apartment-share in a multi-apartment building; no registered
*lietošanas kārtība*; many or unknown/uncooperative co-owners; conversion unrealistic
(can't get unanimity); financing only via non-bank lender; the price discount doesn't
genuinely compensate.

**Lean CONSIDER (with conditions) if:** it's a *pusmāja* / few co-owners; a registered
use-arrangement covers your apartment (or the seller registers one as a condition of sale);
Altum-backed bank financing is confirmed; co-owners are known and amenable to eventual
conversion; and the discount vs. a titled apartment is real enough to pay you for the
residual CL 1074 / creditor-auction exposure that conversion would later remove.

## What to find out about THIS apartment (in priority order)

1. Is the *lietošanas kārtība* **registered in the Zemesgrāmata**, and does it cover your flat?
2. *Pusmāja* or share of a multi-apartment building? How many co-owners / apartments?
3. Zemesgrāmata check: encumbrances/debts (hipotēkas, aizliegumi, reālnastas) on the property.
4. Who are the other co-owners — solvent? reachable? amenable to converting later?
5. Financing: call Altum + a partner bank / broker — will they fund *this* share, at what LTV?
6. The discount vs. an equivalent titled apartment — is it big enough to pay for the risk?
