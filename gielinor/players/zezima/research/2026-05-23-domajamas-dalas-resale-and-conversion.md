# Domājamās daļas — resale, liquidity & conversion to dzīvokļa īpašums

**Question.** For a Riga apartment held as a *domājamā daļa* (ideal/notional share of a co-owned property, *kopīpašums*) rather than a registered separate apartment (*dzīvokļa īpašums*):
- **A.** How does ideal-share ownership affect a future sale — price discount vs proper apartment, buyer-pool size, pre-emption rights of co-owners?
- **B.** Can a *domājamās daļas* property be converted into proper *dzīvokļa īpašums* — legal process, blockers, cost/time, who can drive it?

**Date of research.** 2026-05-23.

**Confidence.** Medium. The *mechanics* (pre-emption rights, the conversion process, unanimity requirement, VZD fees) are well-anchored from primary/official and reputable explainer sources, high confidence. The *market-magnitude* claims (how big the discount is, how small the buyer pool) are weakly sourced — Latvian portals consistently say "shares are worth less and harder to sell" but rarely put a percentage on it; the one concrete number I found is an extreme anecdote. Treat discount magnitude as directional, not precise. The full cadastral-survey cost is a known gap (VZD only gives it via a calculator after inspection).

## Findings

### A. Resale / liquidity / value

**A1. There IS a discount, and every Latvian source agrees on the direction — but magnitude is poorly quantified.**

The consistent line across portals is that an ideal share sells *below* an equivalent registered apartment. city24 states it plainly: the market value of a unit in an undivided building *"būs zemāka par analoga dzīvokļa īpašuma vērtību"* — will be lower than the value of an equivalent (registered) apartment property, and *"buyers and banks view ownership of domājamās daļas with suspicion due to co-ownership risks"* (city24.lv). LV portāls (2021) frames it as a property you shouldn't buy at all if the documentation isn't sorted: *"Ja no šiem aspektiem nav sakārtots īpašums, tad nav ieteicams iegādāties domājamās daļas."*

On **magnitude**, I could not find a clean market figure (e.g. "shares trade 20–40% below"). The one concrete data point is an extreme anecdote from an LV portāls valuation consultation (2019): an owner reports *"'Latio' dzīvokli novērtējis par aptuveni 30 000 eiro, bet nekustamo īpašumu firma man piedāvā aptuveni 2500 eiro"* — Latio valued the apartment at ~€30 000, but a real-estate firm offered ~€2 500 for the share. That is roughly a 90%+ haircut, but it is a single anecdote and almost certainly reflects a *proportional* valuation of an undivided whole rather than a real comparable for a usable apartment (see A2). *Do not generalise the 90% figure.* My honest read *(inference from the qualitative consensus + the valuation mechanism)*: for a *usable, occupied* apartment held as a share with a clean use-arrangement, expect a meaningful but not catastrophic discount — directionally in the tens-of-percent range — driven mostly by the mortgage problem and the smaller buyer pool, not by the proportional-valuation artifact. This is an estimate, not a sourced number.

**A2. Why the discount exists — the valuation mechanism.** When a share is valued "by the book," the valuer values the *whole* co-owned property and then *proportionally* allocates that total across the ideal shares: *"tiek novērtēts viss kopīpašums un tiek proporcionāli izdalīta konkrēto domājamo daļu vērtība, tāpēc tā ir neliela"* (LV portāls 2019). A proportional slice of a whole building, with no exclusive right to a specific apartment attached on paper, prices low. The real-world price is set instead by what a buyer will pay for the *use* of a specific apartment — but that buyer faces the constraints below, which is what actually depresses the price.

**A3. The buyer pool is smaller — and the mortgage problem is the main driver.** Banks are reluctant to lend against an ideal share: a loan against the *house* can only be issued with the consent of *all* co-owners, so a single co-owner generally cannot mortgage their share unilaterally (search synthesis; this is P2's territory — noted lightly, not deep-researched here). The practical consequence for resale: **cash buyers dominate.** That collapses the buyer pool versus a normal apartment (where a buyer can get a standard mortgage), which is the real liquidity penalty. city24 makes the inverse point — once divided into proper apartments, *"banks more readily finance"* and the owner can sell *"bez ierobežojumiem jebkurā laikā"* (without restriction, at any time). *(The detailed mortgage mechanics are P2's brief — flagged, not duplicated.)*

**A4. Exit complication — co-owners' pre-emption + redemption rights.** This is the hardest-anchored part of section A. Selling a share to an outsider is a two-stage gauntlet:

- **First refusal (pirmpirkuma tiesības), 2 months.** The share must first be offered to the other co-owners. In practice the seller signs a purchase contract with the outside buyer first, then presents it to the co-owners; they have **two months** to decide whether to buy on the *same terms*. *"Pārdodamā domājamā daļa vispirms jāpiedāvā pārējiem kopīpašniekiem. Tikai tad, ja viņi pēc divu mēnešu domāšanas no pirmpirkuma tiesībām atsakās..."* (pardotipasumu.lv). The 2-month clock starts when they receive written notice / a copy of the contract (LV portāls 2021).
- **Redemption right (izpirkuma tiesība), 1 year.** Even after a sale to an outsider completes, a co-owner can **reclaim** the share within **one year** of the new owner being registered in the Land Register (pardotipasumu.lv). This is a real overhang: it makes the title less clean for a year post-purchase, which itself can deter buyers.
- Net effect: selling a share is *"sarežģītāks, nekā pārdodot dzīvokļa īpašumu"* — more complicated than selling a registered apartment (pardotipasumu.lv). Coordination with co-owners, statutory waiting periods, and the one-year clawback all add friction and time.

**A5. Other resale-depressing factors.** Absence of a Land-Register-registered use-arrangement (*lietošanas kārtības līgums*) creates legal ambiguity over which apartment "your share" actually entitles you to use; mismatch between registered shares and actual rooms; and the standing risk that *any* co-owner can demand partition / forced dissolution (LV portāls 2021). Each adds title risk that buyers price in.

### B. Conversion to dzīvokļa īpašums

**B1. Yes, it is the standard, legally-blessed cure — and the State actively eased it in 2021.** Converting *kopīpašums / domājamās daļas* into separate *dzīvokļa īpašumi* is exactly what the *Dzīvokļa īpašuma likums* + VZD division process is for. As of **2021-08-05**, the rules were *relaxed*: previously a building could be divided into apartments only if it was the *sole* dwelling on the plot; now one or several residential buildings on a single plot in one property can be divided (all residential buildings on that plot must be divided **simultaneously**) (VZD /pdzil). The reform also lets an individual apartment owner do construction work in their own unit/common parts without all-owner sign-off *if* it doesn't affect other buildings/land or change the ideal shares.

**B2. The process (prerequisites → VZD opinion → registration).** Both city24 and the Vaska law firm give the same four prerequisites the building must satisfy *before* VZD will issue its opinion:
1. Building commissioned / in use — *"Ēkai ir jābūt nodotai ekspluatācijā."*
2. Cadastrally surveyed — *"kadastrāli uzmērītai"* — i.e. **kadastrālā uzmērīšana** done.
3. All room groups registered in the Cadastre — *"kadastrā reģistrējot visas ēkā esošās telpu grupas"*; floor plans must exist in digital form (VZD).
4. Building registered in the Land Register — *"ēkai ir jābūt reģistrētai zemesgrāmatā."*

Then: all co-owners file the application + a written agreement to divide → VZD prepares the **atzinums** (opinion on the division and the calculation of the ideal shares belonging to each apartment) → each apartment is registered in the Cadastre → finally each *dzīvokļa īpašums* gets its **own separate Land-Register section** (*"katrs dzīvokļa īpašums...tiek reģistrēts zemesgrāmatā, tam atverot atsevišķu zemesgrāmatas nodalījumu"* — city24). At that point each owner can act independently and cannot be forced to remain in co-ownership.

**B3. The killer blocker — unanimity.** Division requires the consent of **all** co-owners. Both the law firm and city24 stress it, with the emphatic parenthetical: *"darbības ar kopīpašumu...veicamas tikai ar visu (!) kopīpašnieku piekrišanu."* One co-owner refusing — or simply being unreachable / abroad — halts the whole thing: *"lēmuma pieņemšana izpaliek,"* and the cooperative co-owners become *"ķīlnieki"* (hostages) to the holdout. VZD's own pages repeat that the application must come *"no īpašnieka vai visiem kopīpašniekiem"* (from the owner, or from all co-owners). **So a single new co-owner cannot drive the conversion alone — it needs unanimity.**

**B4. The escape clause if unanimity fails.** No co-owner can be *forced to remain* in co-ownership — *"nevienu kopīpašnieku nevar piespiest palikt kopīpašumā."* Any co-owner can demand termination of co-ownership at any time, without giving reasons. If voluntary agreement fails, a co-owner can sue for **forced dissolution** of the co-ownership; recent Civil Law amendments make *division into apartment ownerships* the *preferred* court outcome where physical division of the property is impossible (city24). So the practical path for a single owner who wants out / wants the building divided over a holdout's objection is: **court action for dissolution**, not a unilateral VZD application. That is slower, costlier, and adversarial — but it exists.

**B5. Cost (the VZD slice is cheap; the survey is the variable).** Concrete published figures:
- **VZD atzinums** (opinion on division + ideal-share calculation): **€40.30** (LV portāls 2022; VZD). Note one consultation cited the per-apartment cadastre-registration fee at **€44.50 × number of apartments** (*"maksa par katra jauna dzīvokļa īpašuma reģistrācija...44,50 eiro"*, LV portāls 2022), while an earlier/related VZD answer cited **€30.00 per new apartment**. Treat ~€30–45 per apartment as the order of magnitude; confirm the current figure against the VZD price list (https://www.vzd.gov.lv/lv/cenradis-un-samaksas-kartiba). These VZD fees assume the cadastral survey is **already done.**
- **Kadastrālā uzmērīšana (cadastral survey)** — the big variable and a **gap**. VZD does not publish a flat price; it's set per MK noteikumi Nr. 787 (2015-12-22) and computed by VZD's online calculator based on the actual scope, with the final fee fixed only after the work: *"aptuvenajai maksai...ir tikai informatīva nozīme, un tā var atšķirties no reālās maksas."* If the building/units were never surveyed, this is likely the dominant cost line (private surveyors, per-m² / per-unit). Not quantified by the sources read.
- **Not surfaced:** notary fees, Zemesgrāmata state fee (*kancelejas nodeva* — typically a small % of value), and any architect/technical work to get the building "commissioned" if it isn't. Both explainers concede only that division *"saistīta gan ar laika, gan naudas patēriņu"* (involves both time and money) without numbers.

**B6. Time.** No source gave a timeline. *(Inference)*: the rate-limiters are (a) getting unanimous co-owner agreement (can be the longest pole — social, not procedural), (b) the cadastral survey if not yet done (weeks-to-months of surveyor scheduling + processing), and (c) the VZD atzinums + Zemesgrāmata registration (administrative, weeks each). If all paperwork prerequisites are already in place and everyone agrees, the VZD-plus-registration tail is plausibly a couple of months; if a survey is needed or a co-owner must be brought onside, it stretches considerably. Mark this as estimate.

**B7. Why bother converting (the upside for Zezima's resale question).** Conversion directly fixes section A: each unit gets its own Land-Register section → banks will mortgage it → buyer pool expands → no more 2-month pre-emption gauntlet or 1-year clawback → market value rises to the level of *"analoga dzīvokļa īpašuma."* city24 also flags a tax angle: Riga (and other municipalities) can apply *higher* property-tax rates to undivided buildings (the reduced rate covers only ~30 m² per registered person, with 1.5% on the remainder), so division can also lower the annual NĪN bill. So conversion is the lever that turns a hard-to-sell, cash-only, discounted share into a normal liquid apartment — *if* the co-owners can be brought to unanimous agreement (or forced via court).

## Gaps & open questions

- **Market-discount magnitude is the biggest soft spot.** Every source agrees shares sell below registered apartments; none gives a defensible market percentage for a *usable* apartment-as-share. The only number (≈90%) is an unrepresentative proportional-valuation anecdote. A real answer would need either a broker's rule-of-thumb or scraped ss.lv/city24 comparables (share-listings vs equivalent registered apartments in the same Riga building/area). Worth a follow-up if the number matters to a decision.
- **Two paywalled/blocked canonical sources.** santa.lv (adv. Radziņš, "why not buy shares") is behind SANTA+; LSM ("time bomb") returned 403. Both are likely the richest qualitative reads — Niklavs can open them directly. The Kudeikina RSU dissertation is the rigorous academic source if a legal-grade answer is ever needed.
- **Cadastral-survey cost unquantified.** Likely the dominant conversion cost line if the building/units were never surveyed. Needs VZD's calculator (with actual building parameters) or a private surveyor quote.
- **Per-apartment VZD fee figure conflicts** between sources (€30 vs €44.50). Reconcile against the live VZD price list before quoting a number.
- **Whole-building scope.** The 2021 reform requires *all* residential buildings on the plot to be divided simultaneously — so for a multi-building plot the conversion can't be done piecemeal for just one building. Relevant if Zezima's property sits on a shared plot with other structures.
- **Notary / Zemesgrāmata kancelejas nodeva** for opening the new sections not quantified here.
- **Mortgage-on-share specifics** deliberately left to P2; I only confirmed the directional fact (banks generally won't lend on a unilateral share) because it's the main driver of the resale discount in section A.
