# Cross-checking two derivations of the same quantity catches self-introduced bugs

**Observation ([[S124_61d62e21_shipping-agent-report|S124]], 2026-06-01, sid b82b0b90).** While building the shipping report's §1 evidence layer, I shipped a bug: `(cost_source == 'invoice').mean()` in polars **propagates NULL** on the 62,743 NULL-`cost_source` rows, and `.mean()` then **drops those nulls from the denominator** — inflating % invoiced (ORWO read 77.2% vs the true 71.9%). I didn't catch it by re-reading the code. I caught it because the **same quantity appeared two ways in the report** — the §1 segment table (ORWO 77.2%) and the ORWO-by-carrier table (which weighted out to ~72%) — and the two disagreed. Same rows, two numbers → one is wrong.

**The rule.** When a deterministic build computes the same figure through two independent paths, **render both and check they agree** — the disagreement is a free correctness probe that no amount of code re-reading provides. Here it was segment-level vs carrier-weighted % invoiced; the principle generalizes (a total vs the sum of its parts, a headline vs the detail that rolls up to it). Build the redundancy in deliberately when the number is load-bearing.

**Pairs with the nullable-column family** ([[2026-06-01-verify-diffs-both-ways-and-explicit-presence-flags]]): the root cause was again a **nullable column** (`cost_source`) silently changing an aggregate's meaning — `.mean()` over a null-propagating boolean is not "% true," it's "% true among non-null." Fix: `.fill_null(False)` before aggregating a boolean over a nullable column. The cross-check is what surfaced it; the nullable-column wariness is what explains it.
