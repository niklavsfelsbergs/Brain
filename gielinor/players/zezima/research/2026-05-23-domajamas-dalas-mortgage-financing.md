# Research — Mortgage / bank financing for a *domājamā daļa* (ideal share of co-owned property) in Latvia

**Question.** Will Latvian banks issue a residential mortgage (*hipotekārais kredīts*) to finance the purchase of a *domājamā daļa* — a notional/ideal share of a co-owned property (*kopīpašums*) — as opposed to a registered separate apartment (*dzīvokļa īpašums*)? If so, on what conditions; can the share be pledged on its own in the *Zemesgrāmata*; and what's the practical upshot for a buyer?

**Date of research.** 2026-05-23.

**Confidence.** Medium-high. The *legal* mechanics (a co-owner may pledge their own share without consent) are clearly anchored in primary-aligned sources (LV portāls citing Civillikums). The *bank-practice* picture (banks generally decline a bare ideal share, prefer/require a registered separate unit) is well-supported by a broker statement and practitioner/news coverage, but **no major bank's website states an explicit "we do / don't lend against a *domājamā daļa*" rule** — that part is inference from convergent secondary sources plus the absence of any contrary bank statement. Altum's explicit support for *domājamā daļa* purchases is high-confidence (direct quote from altum.lv).

## Findings

### 1. Can an ideal share be pledged/mortgaged on its own in the Zemesgrāmata? — **Yes, legally.**

Under **Civillikums Art. 1072**, each co-owner owns their *domājamā daļa* outright and may dispose of it — sell or mortgage — *"according to its nature, provided it does not affect the other co-owners' shares,"* and crucially **without the consent of the other co-owners** (LV portāls 2021, both articles). The pledge is registered against that owner's share in the *Zemesgrāmata*. On default, **only that owner's ideal share is auctioned**, not the whole property (LV portāls "Vai ... drīkst ieķīlāt," 2021). If the co-ownership is later divided into separate units (e.g. *dzīvokļa īpašumi*), the mortgage **follows** the share onto the replacement unit (AT case-law classifier, Civillikums ķīlas tiesība).

So the *legal instrument* exists: an ideal share is independently pledgeable collateral. The constraint is not the law — it's bank appetite.

### 2. Will the major banks actually lend against a bare ideal share? — **Generally no / very reluctantly; practice strongly favors a registered separate unit.** *(inferred from convergent secondary sources + bank silence)*

- The clearest lender-side statement found is from a secured lender (eLizings), not a Tier-1 bank, and it is a flat **no for a whole-house loan on co-owned property without all owners' consent**: *"kredīts pret mājas ķīlu var tikt izsniegts tikai ar visu īpašnieku piekrišanu."* This is the *whole-property* mortgage case (mortgage the entire house, which legally requires every co-owner to sign). It is **distinct from** pledging just your own share — but it captures why banks dislike *kopīpašums*: clean foreclosure on a fraction is messy.
- LSM's specialist coverage states banks **have difficulty financing ideal-share structures** and frames buying *domājamās daļas* as "a time bomb" — explicitly because such shares are hard to later convert/divide into *dzīvokļa īpašumi*, which is what banks want as clean collateral.
- None of the Tier-1 bank mortgage pages (Swedbank, SEB, Luminor, Citadele) examined state an explicit positive rule for lending against a *domājamā daļa*. Citadele specifies only "RE located in Latvia" + mandatory valuation and is **silent** on share-structured collateral. Swedbank's published rejection reasons are entirely borrower-financial, not collateral-form. The **absence of any "yes, we lend against ideal shares" statement**, combined with the practitioner consensus, is the basis for the *(inferred)* "generally no" — banks default to wanting a registered, separately foreclosable unit.

**Bottom line on banks:** financing a *dzīvokļa īpašums* is the normal, frictionless case. Financing a *domājamā daļa* is at best **case-by-case and harder**, at worst **declined** — especially the riskier sub-case of an "apartment" that is really an undivided share in a multi-apartment building. A *pusmāja* (half of a clearly-divisible two-part house) with a clean use arrangement is the most financeable sub-case.

### 3. If they do lend — what conditions differ?

- **Consent.** Pledging *your own* share: no co-owner consent needed (Civillikums 1072). Mortgaging the *whole property*: **all co-owners must consent and sign** (eLizings). Banks generally want the cleaner of the two; for a share purchase they look hard at the co-ownership setup. *(inferred)*
- **Lietošanas kārtība (use arrangement).** Strongly recommended — and a registered one materially de-risks the deal. LV portāls (2017): if the use arrangement is **entered in the Zemesgrāmata**, *"tas jau ir drošāk"* — co-ownership relations are settled, disputes pre-empted. A bank assessing share collateral will view a registered *lietošanas kārtība* as a positive (a fraction with a defined, exclusive physical area is closer to a real unit). *(inferred from the risk framing; the article ties the arrangement to safety, not explicitly to bank approval)*
- **What gets mortgaged.** Either (a) the buyer's own ideal share alone (legally clean, but the collateral the bank ends up holding is a fraction → less attractive), or (b) the whole property with all owners signing (cleaner collateral, needs everyone). *(inferred)*
- **LTV / interest premium.** No source gave a *domājamā-daļa-specific* LTV cap or rate premium. General mortgage LTV is ~80–85% (up to 95% with Altum). For an ideal share, expect **tighter LTV / larger down payment** if financed at all, and possibly a routing toward the secured-lender / non-bank segment (eLizings-type, up to 80%, cap €250k) rather than a Tier-1 mortgage. **This is the largest gap — flagged below.** *(inferred / unconfirmed)*

### 4. Altum (state-backed guarantee) — the notable exception. **Explicitly supports buying a *domājamā daļa*.**

Altum's **family-housing guarantee** (and parallel young-specialist / regional programs) explicitly states the residential premises **can be ideal shares**: *"Dzīvojamā telpa var būt arī domājamās daļas ... otras pusmājas daļas iegāde programmas ietvaros tiek atbalstīta."* So the canonical financeable case — **buying the other half of a half-house (pusmāja)** — is directly covered. The Altum guarantee is delivered *through* partner banks (Swedbank, Luminor, SEB, Citadele have cooperation agreements), so an Altum-guaranteed loan for a *domājamā daļa* is the most concrete realistic path to a bank-issued mortgage on an ideal share. Guarantee 5–30% (Rīga/Pierīga), up to 50% (regions); max loan €300k; down payment can drop toward ~5%.

### 5. Practical upshot for a buyer

- **Realistic but harder, not "freely available."** A *domājamā daļa* is **not** the frictionless standard case that a registered *dzīvokļa īpašums* is.
- **Best path:** a clean *pusmāja* / clearly-divisible share **with a *lietošanas kārtība* registered in the Zemesgrāmata**, financed via an **Altum-guaranteed** loan through a partner bank. That combination directly fits Altum's stated support and gives the bank the comfort it needs.
- **Hardest / often-declined case:** an "apartment" that is actually an undivided ideal share in a multi-apartment building (the "time bomb"). Banks resist; conversion to *dzīvokļa īpašums* later is uncertain.
- **Fallback if Tier-1 banks decline:** non-bank secured lenders (eLizings-type) will lend against RE up to ~80% market value (cap ~€250k), and a single co-owner can pledge their own share without others' consent — so financing *can* be arranged even where a mainstream mortgage isn't, at less favorable terms.
- **Always check** the co-owner relationship and whether the use arrangement is registered before counting on financing.

## Gaps & open questions

- **No bank-specific written policy.** None of Swedbank / SEB / Luminor / Citadele publish an explicit "we lend / don't lend against a *domājamā daļa*" rule. The "generally no / case-by-case" conclusion is **inferred** from one secondary lender statement + practitioner consensus + bank silence. A direct call to each bank's mortgage desk (or a broker like hipotekas.lv) would convert this from medium to high confidence.
- **LTV / rate premium for ideal shares — unquantified.** No source gave a *domājamā-daļa*-specific LTV cap or interest premium. The "tighter LTV, possibly non-bank routing" is inference. Unknown whether a Tier-1 bank with an Altum guarantee applies the standard 95%-with-guarantee LTV to a *pusmāja* purchase or haircuts it.
- **LSM "time bomb" article date + the Civil Law amendment month/year** could not be pinned (page returned HTTP 403; relied on search summary). The amendment referenced ("in May") that "addressed some issues" with multi-apartment ideal-share sales is worth dating precisely if this matters — current practice may already be shifting.
- **Altum fine print for ideal shares.** Altum confirms ideal shares qualify in principle, but whether every partner bank will originate an Altum-guaranteed loan specifically against an ideal share (vs. only against fully-registered units) is unconfirmed — the guarantee program permits it; individual bank underwriting may still balk.
- **Court-practice nuance** (mortgage following the share onto a post-division unit) is cited from a search summary of the AT classifier, not a deep read of a specific judgment.
