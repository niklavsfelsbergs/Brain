# Domājamās daļas — practical risks of buying a Riga apartment as a co-ownership share, and the pre-purchase due-diligence checklist

**Question.** What are the practical, real-world risks and obligations of buying and living in a Riga apartment held as a *domājamā daļa* (notional/ideal share of a co-owned property, *kopīpašums*) rather than as a registered separate apartment (*dzīvokļa īpašums*) — and what do Latvian practitioners, notaries, and buyer-guidance sources advise checking BEFORE purchase?

**Date of research.** 2026-05-23.

**Confidence.** Medium-high. Practitioner consensus is strong and convergent across agencies, law firms, and the state legal portal (lvportals.lv). One load-bearing item — the utility-liability rule — rests on a *recent* Senāts ruling (SKC-108/2025, Dec 2025) that may not yet be fully settled in lower-court practice. Some agency pieces are SEO/marketing-flavoured; weighted accordingly. Out of scope here: formal Civillikums article-by-article rights (P1), mortgage financing (P2), resale price / conversion to *dzīvokļa īpašums* (P3).

## Findings

### What a *domājamā daļa* actually is, and why so many Riga apartments are sold this way

A *kopīpašums* (co-ownership) means several people own one indivisible property; each holds a *domājamā daļa* — a notional/ideal fraction (e.g. 1/2, 234/10000) of the *whole* thing, not a physically delineated unit. Per KIVI, a great many Latvian multi-apartment buildings — especially Soviet-era stock (1955–1990) — were **never legally split into separate apartment properties** (*dzīvokļa īpašumi*), so "the entire building, including apartments, stairwells, basements and other elements, is legally considered a single property, in which each owner has a 'notional share'" ([KIVI](https://www.kivi.lv/tirgus-jaunumi/kas-ir-kopipasums-dzivoklu-maja-un-ka-tas-veidojas-74)). Buying "an apartment" that's really a *domājamā daļa* means buying a slice of the *building*, with a side-arrangement about which physical rooms you get to use.

The headline practitioner verdict is blunt. Zvērināts advokāts **Egīls Radziņš** advises **not** to buy a *domājamā daļa* in a multi-apartment building, however attractive the offer, because co-ownership is an encumbrance on ownership rights (*īpašumtiesību apgrūtinājums*) that imposes specific duties — and a share-owner can only act on planned transactions if all other co-owners agree (paraphrased from [Santa.lv](https://www.santa.lv/raksts/aktuali/kopipasuma-problemas-jeb-kapec-nepirkt-majas-domajamas-dalas-29498/) snippet; full text paywalled).

### 1. Shared costs & liability — who pays what, and what if a co-owner doesn't

This is the area where the picture **shifted recently**, so it's worth stating the current split carefully. The latest authority is **Senāts SKC-108/2025 (2025-12-17)**, as summarised by Ellex ([Ellex](https://ellex.legal/lv/komunalie-pakalpojumi-jamaksa-kopipasniekam-kas-dzivokli-lieto/)):

- **Communal utilities (heating, hot-water circulation, waste removal, electricity)** — primarily the responsibility of the **co-owner who actually uses/lives in the apartment**, NOT split across all co-owners by share. The court framed these as *usage* expenses: *"Komunālie pakalpojumi primāri ir saistīti ar dzīvokļa lietošanu"* (utilities are primarily linked to use of the apartment).
- **Management / maintenance / reserve-fund (uzkrājuma fonds) costs** — borne by **all co-owners proportionally to their shares**, regardless of who actually uses the apartment, because these preserve value for everyone.
- **Property tax (nekustamā īpašuma nodoklis)** — all co-owners proportionally to shares, regardless of use.
- *Partial exception:* utilities needed purely to preserve the property (minimal heating to prevent damage, security-system power) may be shared, but a co-owner claiming reimbursement carries "a heavy evidentiary burden" to prove preservation-necessity ([Ellex](https://ellex.legal/lv/komunalie-pakalpojumi-jamaksa-kopipasniekam-kas-dzivokli-lieto/)).

The *older* default (pre-2025) was that even management/utility costs split by share regardless of actual use — an appellate court had held that *"arī komunālie maksājumi… ir sadalāmi starp kopīpašniekiem atbilstoši domājamajām daļām neatkarīgi no tā, vai dzīvoklis faktiski lietots"* (utilities split by share regardless of whether the apartment is actually used) ([LV portāls 2016](https://lvportals.lv/e-konsultacijas/9289-komunalie-maksajumi-kopipasuma-2016)). The 2025 Senāts ruling narrows this to management+tax. *(Flag: SKC-108/2025 is recent — lower-court application may still be unsettled.)*

**The practical liability trap.** Utility/maintenance suppliers and the building manager often bill the *property*, not the individual share. If a co-owner doesn't pay their share, the debt attaches to the property and the others feel the pressure — disputes over enforcement and over who-owes-what are among the most common co-ownership conflicts ([REALAT](https://www.realat.lv/lv/bez-kategorijas/riski-perkot-ipasuma-domajamas-dalas-nevis-atsavisku-ipasumu/), [Santa.lv](https://www.santa.lv/raksts/aktuali/kopipasuma-problemas-jeb-kapec-nepirkt-majas-domajamas-dalas-29498/)). A real-world example: shared water losses (unmetered / unverified / late-submitted meter readings) get charged back to the co-owners as a *starpība* (difference) that everyone must cover ([LV portāls search summary](https://lvportals.lv/e-konsultacijas/9289-komunalie-maksajumi-kopipasuma-2016)). *(Inference, well-supported: even though SKC-108/2025 assigns usage-utilities to the user, the supplier's contractual claim against the property and the building manager's bookkeeping mean a non-paying co-owner's debt remains a practical problem for you to chase — the ruling sorts out who-owes-whom *internally*, not who the supplier can pursue.)*

### 2. Joint decision-making — the single-uncooperative-co-owner problem

The core constraint: acting on the *object* of the co-ownership requires the consent of **all** co-owners (Civillikums Art. 1068 framing, per [LV portāls](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/328256-kas-jazina-iegadajoties-dalas-kopipasuma-2021)). Practitioners describe the lived consequence:

- **Limited control.** Buying only a share means "parasti nav pilnas kontroles pār īpašuma izmantošanu" — no full control over use; decisions on repairs, improvements, or renting can require the others' consent ([REALAT](https://www.realat.lv/lv/bez-kategorijas/riski-perkot-ipasuma-domajamas-dalas-nevis-atsavisku-ipasumu/)).
- **Renovations / major repairs / changing use / renting out** typically need unanimity or at least a majority (*"visu līdzīpašnieku vienprātība vai vismaz balsu vairākums"*) ([REALAT](https://www.realat.lv/lv/bez-kategorijas/riski-perkot-ipasuma-domajamas-dalas-nevis-atsavisku-ipasumu/)).
- **One uncooperative co-owner can block everything** — repairs you need, a renovation, a development project — and there is no fast remedy short of going to court. KIVI lists "lack of unanimity in management decisions" and disputes over shared spaces (basements, attics, heating) as standard friction ([KIVI](https://www.kivi.lv/tirgus-jaunumi/kas-ir-kopipasums-dzivoklu-maja-un-ka-tas-veidojas-74)).

The mitigant is a **lietošanas kārtības līgums** (use-arrangement agreement) — see the checklist below. Without one, *"bieži rodas domstarpības"* (disputes frequently arise) ([LV portāls search summary](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/328256-kas-jazina-iegadajoties-dalas-kopipasuma-2021)).

### 3. Worst-case scenarios

**(a) A co-owner forces the sale/auction of the WHOLE property.** No co-owner can be forced to remain in co-ownership; each may *at any time* demand division (*"nevienu kopīpašnieku nevar piespiest palikt kopīpašumā… katrs kopīpašnieks var katrā laikā prasīt… dalīšanu"*) ([LV portāls auction explainer](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/311175-kad-kopipasumu-izbeidz-to-izsolot-2019)). If co-owners can't agree on the other two division methods (real partition, or buy-out by one with cash to the others), **the court orders the whole thing sold at auction**. The auction starting price is the **forced-sale value, typically 70–75% of market value**, and proceeds are split by share after deducting execution costs ([LV portāls](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/311175-kad-kopipasumu-izbeidz-to-izsolot-2019)). So a single co-owner you don't control can trigger a below-market sale of your home. *(Note for cross-read with P1: 2022 amendments added a 5-year bar on demanding division of residential co-ownership absent an important reason, and made apartment-property division the primary form — surfaced in the English search; legal detail is P1's territory.)*

**(b) A co-owner's share is seized by THEIR creditors → a stranger becomes your co-owner.** A *domājamā daļa* is itself an object of civil circulation — it can be pledged, mortgaged, inherited, and **seized for the share-owner's own debts**. If a co-owner's share is auctioned by *their* creditors, whoever buys it becomes your new co-owner. Crucially, **pre-emption rights do NOT apply at a forced/creditor auction** — *"Pirmpirkuma tiesības… nav izmantojamas, ja īpašums tiek pārdots piespiedu kārtā (izsolē)"* ([LV portāls auction explainer](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/311175-kad-kopipasumu-izbeidz-to-izsolot-2019); confirmed [LV portāls 2024](https://lvportals.lv/e-konsultacijas/34579-vai-piespiedu-izsole-kopipasniekam-ir-pirmpirkuma-tiesibas-2024)). So you can end up co-owning your building with an unknown investor or debt-buyer with no say in who that is. (For ordinary *voluntary* sales by a co-owner to a non-co-owner, you DO get a two-month pre-emption right — see below.)

**(c) Protracted disputes / litigation.** Poorly-defined rights and obligations → *"ilgstošiem strīdiem vai tiesvedībām"* (long disputes or court cases) is a named risk ([REALAT](https://www.realat.lv/lv/bez-kategorijas/riski-perkot-ipasuma-domajamas-dalas-nevis-atsavisku-ipasumu/)).

**Pre-emption mechanics (the one protection you DO get).** When a co-owner sells their share to a *non*-co-owner, the other co-owners have a **two-month right of first refusal (pirmpirkuma tiesības, Civillikums Art. 1073)** running from receipt of a copy of the purchase agreement. The seller must notify the others with the contract terms; if they're cheated of the chance, they get a **redemption right (izpirkuma tiesības)** to take the share on the same terms ([LV portāls 2022](https://lvportals.lv/e-konsultacijas/25708-par-kopipasnieku-pirmpirkuma-tiesibam-2022)). Note: pre-emption does **not** apply when a co-owner sells to *another existing co-owner* — that sale is free ([LV portāls](https://lvportals.lv/e-konsultacijas/31677-kopipasuma-dalas-pardosanas-nosacijumi-2024)). This protection is also what makes *your own* future resale slower (P3's territory): a buyer must wait out the others' two-month window.

### 4. Pre-purchase due-diligence checklist (what practitioners say to verify BEFORE buying)

Synthesised from the state portal, two agencies, a law firm, and a bank. In rough priority order:

1. **Is there a written AND Land-Register-registered use-arrangement agreement (*lietošanas kārtības līgums* / *koplietošanas līgums*)?** This is the #1 item across every source. It pins down which physical rooms/spaces you actually get to use and how, and (if registered in Zemesgrāmata) binds future owners. Verify it exists, that it covers *your* apartment, and that it's *registered*, not just signed ([LV portāls](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/328256-kas-jazina-iegadajoties-dalas-kopipasuma-2021), [KIVI](https://www.kivi.lv/tirgus-jaunumi/kas-ir-kopipasums-dzivoklu-maja-un-ka-tas-veidojas-74), [Luminor](https://www.luminor.lv/lv/kam-pieverst-uzmanibu-pirms-pirkt-majokli)). *Without a registered one, you're buying a fraction of a building with no secured right to a specific apartment.*
2. **Does the access to YOUR specific apartment correspond to the share?** Verify *"vai telpas atbilst domājamām daļām"* — that the rooms actually match the notional share, and that your physical use of the apartment is secured by the arrangement, not just informal practice ([LV portāls](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/328256-kas-jazina-iegadajoties-dalas-kopipasuma-2021), [Luminor](https://www.luminor.lv/lv/kam-pieverst-uzmanibu-pirms-pirkt-majokli)).
3. **Check Zemesgrāmata for encumbrances and debts on the property.** *"Pārbaudīt, vai īpašumam nav apgrūtinājumu (piem., hipotēkas, aizliegumi, reālnastas)"* — mortgages, prohibitions/seizure-bars, and real burdens. Because the property is indivisible, an encumbrance on a co-owner's share is your problem too ([Luminor](https://www.luminor.lv/lv/kam-pieverst-uzmanibu-pirms-pirkt-majokli)).
4. **Find out who the other co-owners are and what they're like.** Their identity and "attitudes" matter — you'll be locked into joint decisions with them, and an insolvent or hostile co-owner is a live risk (see worst-case (a)/(b)) ([KIVI](https://www.kivi.lv/tirgus-jaunumi/kas-ir-kopipasums-dzivoklu-maja-un-ka-tas-veidojas-74)).
5. **Confirm how costs are split and request prior utility bills from the seller.** Understand your proportional management/tax obligations vs usage-based utilities (per SKC-108/2025), and ask the seller for past bills to forecast real running costs ([LV portāls](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/328256-kas-jazina-iegadajoties-dalas-kopipasuma-2021), [Luminor](https://www.luminor.lv/lv/kam-pieverst-uzmanibu-pirms-pirkt-majokli)). Also check for any *existing* arrears the building/co-owners owe.
6. **Verify the property/cadastral status** — check the apartment's status in the cadastral survey file, and that no unauthorised reconstructions were done (these complicate financing and resale) ([LV portāls](https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/328256-kas-jazina-iegadajoties-dalas-kopipasuma-2021), [Luminor](https://www.luminor.lv/lv/kam-pieverst-uzmanibu-pirms-pirkt-majokli)).
7. **Get specialist legal advice before signing.** Both the agency and bank sources explicitly say: consult a real-estate/legal specialist on co-ownership risks before transacting ([KIVI](https://www.kivi.lv/tirgus-jaunumi/kas-ir-kopipasums-dzivoklu-maja-un-ka-tas-veidojas-74), [Luminor](https://www.luminor.lv/lv/kam-pieverst-uzmanibu-pirms-pirkt-majokli)). The strongest practitioner view (Radziņš) is to avoid the structure entirely if you can.

## Gaps & open questions

- **SKC-108/2025 freshness.** The "user pays utilities, share-owners pay management+tax" rule rests on a Dec-2025 Senāts ruling reported in early 2026. It may not yet be uniformly applied by lower courts or by building managers' billing practice. Confidence on the *internal* split is high; on *day-to-day enforcement reality* it's medium.
- **Supplier vs internal liability not fully nailed.** I inferred (well-supported) that a utility supplier / building manager can still pursue the *property* (and thus practically pressure all co-owners) even though SKC-108/2025 assigns usage-utilities to the user internally. I did not find a source stating the supplier's contractual recourse explicitly — worth a targeted check if this matters to a real purchase.
- **Santa.lv full text paywalled.** Radziņš's full reasoning and any concrete case examples weren't readable; only the structural risk list and headline verdict came through the snippet/fetch.
- **Riga-specific data not surfaced.** Sources are Latvia-wide. Whether central-Riga pre-war (pirmskara) buildings carry materially different co-ownership-share risk than Soviet-era stock wasn't separately confirmed (anecdotally pre-war buildings are a common *domājamā daļa* trap, but I didn't source a Riga-specific figure).
- **2022 division-bar interaction.** The 5-year bar on demanding division of residential co-ownership (post-2022 amendments) softens worst-case (a) somewhat, but its exact contours ("important reason," court discretion) are P1 legal-detail territory and not pinned here.
- **Forum vs practitioner.** I deliberately weighted state-portal + law-firm + named-attorney sources over forum/anecdote (likumi.jautajums.lv threads appeared but weren't used as load-bearing). The convergence across independent practitioner sources is itself the confidence signal.
