# Don't mechanize judgment in an analytical monitoring report

**Observation ([[S124_61d62e21_shipping-agent-report|S124]], 2026-06-01).** Designing the weekly shipping report, I reached for a deterministic regression engine — thresholds and baselines deciding what counts as a "fuck-up." The principal corrected the instinct: *"a lot varies and there needs to be human reasoning on top to deduce what might be an issue. Consider it a data analytics task for a senior data analyst who has to check if everything is in order."*

**The lesson.** For an analytical monitoring/report task, operational reality varies too much (mix, weight, zone, fuel, season, one-offs) for thresholds to decide *what's an issue* — a tripwire either spams false positives or gets tuned so loose it misses the real thing. The "is this a problem?" call is judgment, not arithmetic.

**How to apply.** Split the work the right way: the deterministic layer **prepares evidence and ranks attention** (standard cuts, biggest movers, outliers, mix shifts) and makes **zero verdicts**; the judgment — "is this real, is it material, what's the so-what" — stays with the analyst (me), every run, with a running notebook accumulating *what's normal here* + accepted states to gate false positives. The reframe *shrinks* the deterministic ambition and *grows* the judgment layer. Sibling to the mirage-guard discipline (don't trust a mechanical € number without capability + noise-floor checks) and [[D-029_auto-graduate-unambiguous-complete-ready-quests]] (fix the discriminator, not the firmness) — same family: don't encode judgment as a constant.
