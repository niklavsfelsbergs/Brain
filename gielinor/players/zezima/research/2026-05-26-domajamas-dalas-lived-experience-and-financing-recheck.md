# Domājamās daļas — lived experience, sentiment distribution, and a financing re-test

**Subject.** What real owners/buyers/sellers of **multi-apartment** *domājamās daļas* (ideal
shares / *kopīpašums*) apartments in Latvia actually say and experience — the qualitative,
forum-and-community reality — plus a re-test of prior research's claim that banks won't lend on
multi-apartment shares (now that the Ģertrūdes 113 land-register shows five banks doing exactly
that).

**Date.** 2026-05-26. **Author.** Penguin (research operative), Zezima namespace.

**Confidence headline.** *Sentiment distribution: medium-low* — the signal is real and consistent
in its **shape**, but it is **anecdote-and-practitioner-heavy, not survey data**; I did not find a
quantified "X% fine vs Y% nightmare" anywhere, and I refuse to manufacture one from a handful of
posts. *The separator between good and bad outcomes: medium-high* — it converges hard across every
source. *Financing re-test: medium-high and it CORRECTS the prior note's framing* — the
primary land-register evidence plus Altum's own product pages plus the absence of any
"bank-refused-my-share" forum complaint all point the same way. *Two paywalled experts (LSM, santa.lv
Radziņš): still uncracked* — 403 / SANTA+ / dead caches; substance reconstructed from convergent
secondaries, flagged as such.

## The honest meta-finding first (read this before the distribution)

**There is no clean "distribution of lived outcomes" to be had from the open Latvian internet.**
Niklavs wants to know "what do people on the internet actually say." The truthful answer is: the
internet on this topic is dominated by **two voices that are NOT a representative sample** —

1. **Practitioner / advice content** (lawyers, agencies, the state legal portal, news features).
   This is overwhelmingly *cautionary* — "don't, unless." It is written to warn, so it over-weights
   the failure cases. It is the loudest voice and the one prior research already captured.
2. **Legal-Q&A forum posts** (jautajums.lv) — people only post when something has *gone wrong*.
   A satisfied share-owner has no reason to write a forum question. So the forum is a **selection-
   biased complaints box**, not a sentiment poll.

The "it was totally fine" pole is structurally **under-represented online** — content people who
quietly live in a share-flat for fifteen years without incident don't write about. The one clean
first-person "it was fine" account I found (lifeinriga.com) exists only because an expat blogger
happened to document her purchase. So the raw volume of "nightmare" vs "fine" online is **not** the
real-world ratio; it's the ratio of *who-bothers-to-write*. I weight accordingly below and say so
at every turn. This is the single most important caveat in the file.

## 1. Lived-experience / sentiment distribution

### 1a. The two poles, with real accounts

**The "it was fine / navigable" pole.**

- **Lelde (lifeinriga.com), an expat who bought a Riga share-flat ~2013–14.** Her first-person
  account treats the *domājamā daļa* structure and the 2-month *pirmpirkuma* wait as **routine
  bureaucracy, not a hazard**: she explains the share mechanic ("your apartment could be 32/1000,
  the neighbour's 56/1000") matter-of-factly, describes the first-refusal window as a procedural
  delay to registration, and reports **no co-owner conflict, no building dispute, no resale
  drama**. Overall sentiment: *"navigable rather than nightmarish"* — frustrations were the usual
  land-registry-visit bureaucracy, not the share structure.
  https://lifeinriga.com/buying-property-latvia/
  *This is the pole the cautionary literature systematically omits: a normal person living
  normally in a share-flat with nothing to report.*

**The "it became a grind" pole.**

- **Gatis (TVNET feature), bought a share in an ~80-apartment central-Riga building >10 years
  ago.** Attracted by location and a 6th-floor view; bought with denationalization-compensation
  money; **no co-owner exercised first refusal**. What went wrong was **not** the purchase or the
  occupancy — it was **collective governance at scale**: *"80 dzīvokļu ēkā bija grūti vienoties par
  remontdarbiem"* (in an 80-apartment building it was hard to agree on repairs). The building drifted
  until **façade plaster started falling onto the pavement**, which finally forced the residents to
  organise — set up a reserve fund, formalise stewardship. He eventually **sold the share and moved
  to a private house** in Zvejniekciems during COVID; the article frames the sale itself as a known
  hard (*"Vai viegli bija pārdot lietošanā esošo dzīvokli?"* — "was it easy to sell the apartment
  he occupied?").
  https://www.tvnet.lv/8188483/dzivokla-ipasums-vai-domajamas-dalas-kopipasuma-ko-labak-pirkt
  *Note what this account is and isn't: it is NOT "I lost my home" or "a creditor seized a co-owner
  and a stranger moved in." It is "managing an 80-way co-ownership is a slog and selling out took
  effort." A real cost, a bounded one.*

### 1b. The complaints-box (jautajums.lv "domājamās daļas" forum) — what actually goes wrong

The dedicated *domājamās daļas* category at jautajums.lv is the closest thing to a corpus of real
lived disputes. Across ~10 distinct real-person threads, the recurring shapes are
(http://likumi.jautajums.lv/domajamas-dalas):

- **Co-owner behavioural conflict.** *"Otrs līdzīpašnieks laužas manis aizņemtajā istabā un izceļ
  durvis"* — "the other co-owner breaks into the room I occupy and rips out the doors." Lawyer's
  answer: police report, expert damage assessment, claim 50% of repair cost, or sue for court-ordered
  division.
- **Creditor seizure of a co-owner's share.** *"Vai var atņemt 1/2 no dzīvokļa?"* — yes, a co-owner's
  creditors can force-auction *their* share; the other co-owner gets first refusal but cannot stop the
  process. (Matches prior research's worst-case (b).)
- **Selling without consent + the pirmpirkuma/izpirkuma gauntlet** (multiple threads) — you *can*
  sell your share without the others' consent, but they hold first refusal, and if not properly
  offered they can invoke **redemption within a year**.
- **The recurring lawyer refrain** that ties it all together: without a **written, land-register-
  registered *lietošanas kārtība***, *"buyer may end up living with hostile co-owners."* The forum's
  collective verdict, voiced bluntly in a *kopīpašums* thread elsewhere: **"Kopīpašums ir ļaunums"**
  ("co-ownership is evil").

The TVNET case (governance grind) and the jautajums cases (disputes, seizures) together describe the
**texture** of share trouble: it is **co-owner-relations and collective-decision trouble**, almost
never "the legal structure failed me on day one."

### 1c. The distribution — characterized honestly, not quantified

I will not give a fake percentage. What the evidence *does* support, as a **shape**:

- **The modal lived outcome is uneventful** — most share-owners live in their flat without the
  worst-case ever firing. The cautionary literature concedes this implicitly (its warnings are about
  *risk*, i.e. things that *can* happen, not things that *usually* happen). The CL 1074 forced-
  division and creditor-auction scenarios are **real but rare** — they appear as *advice-column
  hypotheticals and isolated disputes*, not as a drumbeat of "it happened to me" testimony.
- **The common, non-catastrophic complaint is governance friction** — slow/blocked repairs, free-
  riding co-owners, the hassle of needing consent. This is the Gatis pole and it is genuinely
  *frequent* in large old buildings.
- **The catastrophic outcomes** (lose-the-home forced auction; a debt-buyer becomes your co-owner;
  trapped by a holdout) are **what the literature is most afraid of and what the forum occasionally
  evidences**, but they are the **tail**, not the body. They cluster in *unsorted* shares — no use-
  order, absent/insolvent/hostile co-owners, denationalization-era messes.

So: **mostly fine-to-annoying, with a real and nameable bad tail.** The online volume over-weights
the tail because of who writes; the lived base rate is milder than the internet's tone.

### 1d. The separator — THE most decision-relevant finding

Every source that distinguishes good from bad outcomes converges on the **same** small set of
discriminators. This is the heart of the brief's question:

| Separator | "Totally fine" side | "Nightmare" side |
|---|---|---|
| **Registered *lietošanas kārtība*** | present — exclusive use of your flat binds successors | absent — your right to a specific flat is informal, contested |
| **Functioning manager / *biedrība*** | yes — repairs, reserve fund, billing actually happen | no — drift until plaster falls (Gatis) |
| **Co-owner relations & solvency** | known, reachable, paying | hostile, absent abroad, or insolvent (seizure risk) |
| **Building scale / type** | *pusmāja* or few co-owners — easy consensus | 80-way old rental house — consensus near-impossible |
| **Financing obtained** | a bank lent → normal buyer, normal resale pool | cash-only → trapped, deep discount |

The TEGOS law-firm piece states the resale corollary directly: shares **with** a registered
*kopīpašuma lietošanas kārtības līgums* are *"biežāk ir darījumu objekts"* — **more frequently
actually transact** — than shares without one. https://tegos.legal/lv/publikacija/kopipasums-ka-pardot/

**This matters enormously for Niklavs's case**, because the Ģertrūdes 113 folio read (prior bank
draft) already established that flat 41 sits on the **"totally fine" side of every single row**:
registered *lietošanas kārtība* (yes), functioning *biedrība* (yes, reg. Feb 2025), attic shares
recalculated and recorded (yes), flat 41 unencumbered bar an ordinary discharged loan (yes), and —
per row 5 — **five banks demonstrably lending in the building** (yes). The lived-experience
research doesn't just *not* contradict the file; it **identifies exactly the checklist his file
already passes** as the thing that separates the calm cases from the horror stories.

## 3. Financing re-test — HIGH VALUE, and it CORRECTS the prior note

**Prior confirmed note's claim** (`bank/notes/.../2026-05-25-domajamas-dalas-risk-model-and-share-
discount.md`): Tier-1 banks resist multi-apartment shares; Altum reaches only *pusmāja*; the pool is
"predominantly cash"; "no evidence lending widened 2025–26" (flagged there as a null result, not a
hard "no").

**The contradicting primary evidence** (Ģertrūdes 113 folio, prior bank draft): **five banks** — SEB,
Swedbank, Luminor, Citadele, BigBank — hold *hipotēkas* registered against **individual apartment-
shares in this exact ~80-unit multi-apartment building, in 2025–2026**, across both old-section and
attic shares.

**What this fresh pass adds, and the verdict.**

- **Altum's own product pages confirm the broader rule.** Altum states residential premises *can be*
  *domājamās daļas*, and — critically, beyond the *pusmāja* case prior research fixated on — that its
  multi-apartment programs apply to apartment owners **"both when apartments are registered as
  individual properties and when they are co-owned (unsplit)"**, with co-owned-building maintenance
  decisions taken by simple majority. https://www.altum.lv/pakalpojumi/iedzivotajiem/majoklu-garantija-gimenem-ar-berniem/
  ; https://www.altum.lv/pakalpojumi/iedzivotajiem/ — *so the "Altum is pusmāja-only" read in the
  prior note is too narrow.*
- **No bank publishes a "we don't lend on shares" rule.** I re-checked Swedbank, SEB, Luminor,
  Citadele mortgage pages (https://www.swedbank.lv/private/credit/loans/home ;
  https://www.seb.lv/en/private/loans/home-loan ; https://www.luminor.lv/en/private/housing-loan ;
  https://www.citadele.lv/en/private/mortgage/). All four are silent on ownership structure; they
  gate on borrower finances + Latvian RE + valuation. Silence is not a denial — and these four are
  named the most foreigner-friendly lenders in 2026 (Investropa, https://investropa.com/blogs/news/latvia-us-citizen).
- **The forum "bank refused my share" complaint does not exist in volume.** I searched specifically
  for refusal/rejection anecdotes (*"kredīts ... neaizdod / nedeva / atteica ... domājamā daļa"*) and
  found **none** — only general mortgage-rejection threads about borrower income/credit, never
  collateral-structure. If multi-apartment-share refusals were the norm, the complaints box would
  show it. *(Absence of evidence, but pointed absence given how loud the forum is about every other
  share grievance.)*

**Verdict — does it correct the confirmed note? YES, with a precise refinement:**

> The blanket "Tier-1 banks resist multi-apartment shares / predominantly cash / no evidence lending
> widened" is **wrong as stated for functioning, managed, actively-traded buildings**, and **right
> only for the unchecked/dysfunctional median**. The discriminator is the **same separator as the
> lived-experience distribution (§1d)**: banks lend against a share when the file gives them clean
> collateral comfort — a **registered *lietošanas kārtība*** (the share maps to a real, successor-
> bound flat), a functioning manager, reconciled shares, solvent co-owners. Ģertrūdes 113 has all of
> these, which is *why* five banks lent there. The "cash-only" generalisation should be re-scoped to
> *shares without a registered use-order, in dysfunctional or denationalization-mess buildings.*

**Caveat that survives the correction (don't over-claim):** a registered mortgage proves *a* bank
lent against *some* share in the building — it does **not** prove the LTV offered, the haircut, or how
hard the borrower had to push, and it doesn't guarantee Niklavs's own bank will lend on *his* share at
a good LTV. The building-level base rate is firmly "financeable, not cash-only"; **the personal step —
his own signed bank offer for flat 41 — still stands as the thing to actually obtain.** The correction
is to the *generalisation*, not to the *to-do*.

## 4. Pirmpirkuma (2-mo first refusal) / izpirkuma (1-yr redemption) in practice

**How often do they actually delay or kill deals? Verdict: a delay-and-uncertainty tax that rarely
fires, not a deal-killer — confirmed by practice sources, consistent with prior research.**

- **Mechanics in practice** (LV portāls consultations): the seller signs with the outside buyer first,
  then serves the contract on the co-owners; they have **two months** to take it *on the same terms*,
  no competitive-bid advantage. https://lvportals.lv/e-konsultacijas/21416-ja-velas-pardot-kopipasuma-dalas-2020
  ; https://lvportals.lv/skaidrojumi/293031-kopipasums-un-ta-zemudens-akmeni-ii-2018
- **Real frequency of co-owners actually exercising it: low.** In **both** lived accounts where a sale
  happened (Gatis selling out; Lelde buying in), **no co-owner exercised first refusal** — the right
  existed and simply lapsed. The forum threads treat it as a procedural step to *comply with* (offer
  it, document the refusal/lapse), not a thing that routinely *fires*. The realistic reason: co-owners
  usually **don't have cash** to match an outside offer.
- **The cost it does impose** is **time and deal-fragility**: ~2 months of overhang during which the
  buyer's commitment can evaporate, plus the **1-year izpirkuma overhang** that keeps the new owner's
  title "not fully clean" for a year (a paper deterrent more than a realised event — I found no
  account of a redemption actually being exercised against a completed good-faith sale; the redemption
  threads are about sales done *secretly / improperly*, which is the situation that actually triggers
  it). https://pardotnekustamo.lv/2024/01/24/pardot-ipasuma-domajamas-dalas/
- **Net:** as a *buyer*, Niklavs's purchase from a single share-seller faces the seller's obligation to
  clear the 2-month window (a scheduling cost, not a risk to him). As a future *seller*, he'd carry the
  same delay + the 1-year clawback overhang on the buyer — part of the resale-friction the discount
  already pays for. Real, bounded, rarely the thing that kills a deal.

## 5. Resale reality

**Verdict: slower and discounted, the consensus is unanimous on direction; achieved-price data is
near-impossible to get from the open web, and the discount magnitude still rests on the asking-price
comps prior research already found. The new colour is qualitative and it confirms the picture.**

- **It's harder and slower — direction unanimous.** *"Domājamās daļas var tikt pārdotas ilgāk"*
  (shares can take longer to sell) against a ~3-month Riga baseline; selling a share is *"sarežģītāks,
  nekā pārdodot dzīvokļa īpašumu"* (more complicated than selling a titled flat). (LV portāls /
  pardotipasumu cluster, via search.)
- **The vivid practitioner line, confirmed circulating:** for a *messy* share, *"nopirks vai nu
  pašnāvnieks, vai mērķtiecīgs biznesmenis"* — **"it'll be bought either by a suicide [someone
  reckless] or a calculating businessman."** This is the buyer-pool collapse stated in folk terms:
  ordinary mortgage-dependent owner-occupiers drop out, leaving reckless buyers or professional
  share-traders. *(Context: this is said about the hard/unsorted end — central-Riga denationalization
  shares with problems — not a clean, use-order-registered, bank-financeable flat.)*
- **The institutional signature of the resale problem:** firms like **pardotnekustamo.lv** advertise
  that they **buy shares "arī problemātiskos gadījumos"** (even in problematic cases), closing *"pat 1
  dienas laikā"* (in as little as a day). https://pardotnekustamo.lv/2024/01/24/pardot-ipasuma-domajamas-dalas/
  The very existence of a "we'll buy your unsellable share" cash-buyer industry is proof that a normal
  open-market sale of a *problematic* share is genuinely hard. *But note the asymmetry:* these firms
  target the *problematic* end; a clean, use-order-registered, bank-financeable share (like flat 41)
  is not their typical inventory — it can sell to a normal buyer, just to a thinner pool and likely
  slower than a titled flat.
- **Achieved (not asking) price data: still a null result.** No open source exposes *closed* share
  transaction prices tagged by ownership structure (VZD medians don't tag it; portal bodies hide the
  share phrase in free text). The **best achieved-price data anywhere remains the Ģertrūdes 113
  folio's own internal sales** (building ceiling ~€1,891/m², flat 86 attic Apr 2026; old renovated
  flats ~€1,826–1,835/m²) — primary, building-specific, already in the prior bank draft, and it
  **corroborates** the external Bruņinieku-28 asking comp (~€1,860/m²). The ~25–45% discount range
  (centred ~a third off) from prior research stands; this pass found nothing to move it, and nothing
  better-sourced to replace the thin comp base. *(The biggest standing gap, unchanged.)*

## What this means for Niklavs's specific checked case

The lived-experience trawl does something more useful than confirm-or-deny the verdict: it **names the
exact checklist that separates the calm cases from the horror stories — and his file passes every
item on it.**

1. **The internet's tone over-states his risk.** The cautionary literature and the complaints-box
   forum are selection-biased toward failure (§meta-finding). The quiet majority who live in share-
   flats uneventfully don't post. His visceral "it sounds dirty" reaction is partly *absorbing the
   tone of a biased corpus*, not measuring his actual exposure.
2. **The bad outcomes are a real but rare tail, and they cluster in *unsorted* shares.** Forced
   auction, a debt-buyer co-owner, holdout-trapped conversion — these are the literature's nightmares
   and they fire mostly where there's **no registered use-order, hostile/insolvent co-owners, a
   dysfunctional or denationalization-mess building**. Flat 41 is the **opposite of that profile** on
   every axis (§1d table): use-order registered, *biedrība* functioning, shares reconciled, co-owners
   actively transacting, five banks lending.
3. **The common complaint — governance friction (the Gatis grind) — is the one real cost he WILL
   carry.** An 80-way co-ownership is a slow, consent-bound thing; repairs and decisions are not his to
   make alone. That's genuine and permanent. But it is the *annoyance* tier, not the *catastrophe*
   tier — and a functioning Feb-2025 *biedrība* with a reserve-fund mechanism is precisely the
   structure that keeps the Gatis "plaster falls before anyone acts" failure from recurring.
4. **Financing fear: substantially downgraded.** The prior note's "cash-only / banks won't lend"
   framing is **corrected** — for a building like this, banks demonstrably do lend (five of them), and
   Altum's own pages cover co-owned multi-apartment buildings. His *to-do* is unchanged (get his own
   signed offer for flat 41), but the *worry* that no bank will touch it is not supported for this
   building.
5. **Resale: slower and discounted, but he is NOT the "pašnāvnieks vai biznesmenis" case.** That line
   is about messy shares. His is a clean, financeable, use-order-registered flat — it will sell to a
   thinner pool, likely slower than a titled flat, at the share discount that's **already in the price
   he's paying** (so he's not also losing it on entry). The discount is the cost of the structure,
   paid once, symmetric in and out.
6. **The two expert "don'ts" (Radziņš, LSM) are calibrated to the median buyer, and what they object
   to is the permanent structural friction — not a checkable defect.** A fair reading: they're right
   that the encumbrance and consent-dependence never go away; they're aimed at people who don't know
   what they're buying. Niklavs knows exactly what he's buying and has checked the file. Their warning
   reduces, for him, to: *only buy if you value this specific flat enough to accept friction you can't
   check away* — which is the values call already on the table.

**One-line for the principal:** the qualitative reality matches the structure of the prior verdict and
sharpens it — the share-flat horror stories are real but they live in *unsorted, dysfunctional* shares,
and flat 41 is a *sorted, functioning, financeable* one; the financing fear specifically is overstated
for this building and the prior note should be re-scoped; what remains is the governance-friction grind
(real, bounded) and the slower/discounted resale (already priced in), i.e. the same values call, with
less fog around it.
